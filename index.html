Visualizing S&P 500 Volatility Regimes

How Federal Reserve policy and market sentiment shape risk across six market sectors

Team Members: Jeremy Sutikno · Qianru Guo · Suprabhat Khanna

Introduction

Market volatility regimes — sustained periods of high or low price turbulence — have dramatic consequences for investors. The 2020 COVID crash erased 34% of the S&P 500's value in just 23 trading days, while the 2022 bear market saw a 25% decline driven by aggressive Federal Reserve rate hikes. Yet these events did not affect all market sectors equally: Technology stocks collapsed during rate hikes while Energy rallied, Healthcare proved defensive, and Financials occupied the middle ground.

This project explores the visual structure of volatility regimes across six major sector ETFs. Using over 15,000 observations spanning a decade of market data (2015–2025), we investigate three core questions: When do volatility regimes form? What economic forces drive them? And how do different market sectors respond? Our five visualizations follow that same narrative order: first we identify when regimes appear over time, then we show which macroeconomic and technical factors help explain them, and finally we compare how sectors move together and how those regime shifts affect portfolio risk and return distributions.

About the Data

Our dataset combines daily price data for six sector ETFs from Yahoo Finance with macroeconomic indicators from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) database. The final cleaned dataset contains 15,822 observations across 6 ETFs, each with 28 engineered features, spanning from October 2015 through November 2025.

Features are organized into five thematic groups:

  • Market prices and returns: daily close price, daily return, 5-day, 20-day, and 63-day price changes (per ETF)
  • Volatility measures: 5-, 10-, 20-, and 50-day realized volatility (annualized), short/long volatility ratio, and short/medium term structure ratio
  • Technical indicators: RSI (14-day), Bollinger Band width and position, ATR ratio, moving average crossover signal, distance from 10-day and 50-day MAs, and 1-, 3-, and 6-month momentum scores
  • Volume signals: 10-day average volume, 50-day average volume, and the ratio between them as a proxy for unusual activity
  • Macroeconomic conditions: VIX (VIXCLS), Federal Funds Rate (DFF), and Unemployment Rate (UNRATE) from FRED, all forward-filled to align with daily trading data

The target variable is a binary volatility regime label: each observation is classified as "High" if its 20-day forward realized volatility exceeds the SPY median (approximately 14.8% annualized), and "Normal" otherwise. For data cleaning, rows with missing values resulting from rolling-window calculations (requiring at least 200 trading days of history) were dropped, and pre-2015 data were excluded due to incomplete FRED series coverage. The FRED macro series were merged by date and forward-filled across weekends and holidays.

Key Terms

Volatility: how much prices move up and down over time. Higher volatility means larger day-to-day swings.

Volatility regime: a period when market returns are relatively calm or unusually turbulent.

Correlation: how similarly two sectors move. Higher correlation means they move more alike.

Drawdown: the percentage drop from a previous peak in portfolio value.

VIX: a commonly used market "fear gauge."

Summary & Conclusions

Through five connected visualizations, this project follows a simple story: first we identify when volatility regimes appear, then we explain what drives them, and finally we show how those shifts affect sector behavior, diversification, and portfolio risk.

1. Volatility regimes can be clearly identified over time

The timeline visualization shows that periods such as the 2020 COVID crash and the 2022 rate-hike cycle were not isolated daily shocks but extended high-volatility regimes. These regimes affected all sectors, but not equally.

2. Volatility regimes are driven more by macroeconomic conditions than short-term technical noise

Our feature-importance chart shows that the Federal Funds Rate and VIX are among the strongest predictors of regime changes. This suggests that policy conditions and market sentiment play a larger role than short-term technical indicators alone.

3. Sector behavior and portfolio risk change substantially during high-volatility periods

Our cross-sector and distribution views show that diversification weakens during market stress, while regime-aware portfolio rules can reduce drawdowns at the cost of only a modest reduction in return. Energy stands out as both the most volatile sector and the clearest example of why regime shifts matter in practice.

Future Directions

Several extensions would strengthen this analysis. First, the regime classifier could be improved by using a hidden Markov model or GARCH-family model instead of a simple median split, which would better capture the persistence of volatility states. Second, the portfolio strategy could be made more realistic by incorporating transaction costs and slippage, which would reduce the apparent advantage of dynamic allocation in high-turnover sectors. Third, expanding to international sector ETFs (European and Asian equivalents) would allow testing whether the correlation convergence effect observed here is a universal phenomenon or specific to U.S. markets. Finally, incorporating earnings announcement dates as a feature could help disentangle idiosyncratic volatility spikes from genuine macro-driven regime shifts.
